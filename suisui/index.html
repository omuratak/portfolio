Water Tracking
App Design

UI/UX design, UX research

Project Summary

Background

Plastic bottles in Japan have a high recycle percentage , but it’s still more eco-friendly to not buy plastic bottles in the first place. Additionally, health benefits of drinking water do not seem to be well established in Japan yet.

Question

How might we help people develop a healthy and eco-friendly habit of drinking water using their own bottles (vs. buying a water bottle)?

Solution

I designed a gamified water app that helps users track their water intake, as well as find nearby water fountains.

Context

This project was a personal side project, so I was free to choose any topic. I wanted to tackle the environmental crisis at an individual level: how can we, as citizens of Earth, partake in eco-friendly behaviors more?

Process

I followed the human centered design approach. I first conducted user interviews to understand eco-friendly behaviors, defined the design problem, and then designed and tested the solution.

1. Understand and Define

We first conducted user interviews (n = 3) to understand eco-friendly behaviors: what kinds of behaviors do people consider “eco-friendly”? What motivates or inhibits eco-friendly behaviors?

After the interview, I created an affinity diagram to qualitatively analyze the results.

From the affinity diagraming exercise, I derived two key insights:

  1. Only a handful of behaviors were consciously labeled as “eco-friendly” in Japan: e.g. conserving electricity, using “my bags” (reusable grocery bags) and “my bottles” (personal water bottles)
  2. It’s difficult to be “eco-friendly” if such behaviors lower their quality of life. Being “eco-friendly” itself may not be a strong enough incentive; users may need something more that align with their interests (e.g. being healthy)

Based on the user research, I came up with the idea of “tracking your water intake to be healthy & eco-friendly”. I did additional desk research　 and found that:

  1. Users buy their own water bottles to save money and be more eco-friendly
  2. Users have experience buying their own water bottles, but fail to continue using it
  3. Users end up buying bottled beverages because they run out of water that they filled in their own bottles

Based on the research, I became confident of my idea for a “water tracking app”, and defined the design problem as such: How might we help people develop a healthy and eco-friendly habit of drinking water using their own bottles (vs. buying a water bottle)?

In thinking of features for the application that can solve the design problem, social comparison & gamification were two ideas that I came up with to build habits and change behavior for the better. Hence, aside from the most basic feature of inputting and tracking your daily water intake, I decided to have additional features (e.g. leaderboard, game) in the app.

2. Design and Evaluate

I first sketched a lo-fi prototype and tested the concept against potential users (n = 2).

Interestingly, I found that having so many “gamification” features were “too much”, and it was not well received. To the users, having a "leaderboard" felt a little overwhelming. Hence, I decided to tone down on the gamification feature and removed the “leaderboard” to simplify the overall app.

In parallel, I defined the visual system for the app. The concept was: simple, clean, and aesthetic.

Finally, I put together a hi-fi prototype using the lo-fi prototype and visual system.

See the final prototype

Reflection

One of the most important aspects about this app “habit building” cannot be tested easily through prototypes created on prototyping tools (e.g. Figma). Rather, it needs to be tested over time to determine whether users can actually build habits that are sustainable.

Another important thing to keep in mind is that the “best interface is no interface” . One of the biggest barriers to continue using this app would be that the user needs to manually input their own water intake. It would be a much better user experience if we can automate the water intake input (e.g. a “smart” water bottle that automatically records water intake).
